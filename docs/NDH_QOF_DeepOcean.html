Standard Operating Procedure

Non-Diabetic Hyperglycaemia
QOF 2025/26

Achieving targets for NDH002 — a guide for administrative and clinical staff at Strouts Place Medical Centre.

Strouts Place Medical Centre Version 1.0 Effective: April 2025 Review: March 2026 Owner: GP Lead
!

Why This Matters

⚠️
Scale & Urgency There were more than 3.7 million people with non-diabetic hyperglycaemia (NDH) in England in 2023/24 — an increase of 530,000 compared with the previous year. NDH (prediabetes) is defined as an HbA1c of 42–47 mmol/mol or a fasting plasma glucose (FPG) of 5.5–6.9 mmol/l. Without intervention, a substantial proportion will progress to type 2 diabetes. Annual monitoring enables early detection of progression so that timely treatment can begin.
ℹ️
Scope NDH002 applies to all patients on the NDH register: adults aged 18 or over with a coded record of non-diabetic hyperglycaemia or pre-diabetes, where that record has not been superseded by a diagnosis of diabetes prior to the start of the financial year. This SOP covers the full annual monitoring pathway — from register identification through blood test completion, coding, NHS DPP referral and QOF monitoring.
💡
NHS Diabetes Prevention Programme (NHS DPP) The NHS DPP is available across England. GP practices can refer patients aged 18 or over with a blood test result demonstrating NDH in the preceding 12 months. Programme completion (attending >60% of sessions) is associated with a 37% relative risk reduction in developing type 2 diabetes. Eligibility, exclusions and referral pathway are detailed in the Process section below.
1

QOF Indicator at a Glance

Code Indicator Points Target
NDH002 The percentage of patients with non-diabetic hyperglycaemia who have had an HbA1c or fasting blood glucose performed in the preceding 12 months 18 50–90%
⚠️
NDH002 — Acceptable tests Either an HbA1c or a fasting blood glucose result in the preceding 12 months satisfies this indicator. Both are acceptable; document whichever test is performed using the correct SNOMED code. Where results indicate progression to diabetes, the patient must be removed from the NDH register and added to the diabetes register.

Roles & Responsibilities

🗂️
Practice Manager
  • Owns overall QOF performance for the NDH indicator
  • Chairs monthly QOF meeting; reviews dashboard and agrees action plans
  • Coordinates annual blood test recall campaign with admin team
  • Escalates persistent non-responders at the monthly QOF meeting
  • Ensures register accuracy is reviewed at least annually with the GP Lead
📋
Admin / Reception Team
  • Runs the NDH register report monthly to identify patients with tests overdue or due within 6 weeks
  • Sends invitation letters and SMS for annual blood test
  • Books phlebotomy or nurse appointments for HbA1c / FBG
  • Files and codes results received from external labs on the same day
  • Logs DNA attempts; escalates patients with 3+ failed contacts to the monthly QOF meeting
🩺
HCA / Practice Nurse
  • Performs or requests the annual HbA1c or fasting blood glucose test
  • Provides brief lifestyle advice at the point of blood test — diet, physical activity, weight
  • Reviews result when available and codes it using the correct SNOMED code on the same day
  • If result indicates diabetes: flags to GP or pharmacist immediately — same day — for formal diagnosis, register change and initiation of management pathway
  • Offers or reinforces NHS DPP referral where the patient is eligible and not yet enrolled
💊
Pharmacist
  • Attends monthly QOF meeting
  • Acts on results indicating progression to diabetes when flagged by HCA — confirms with GP, supports initiation of management pathway and register change
  • Reviews medication lists for patients with NDH — flags drugs associated with hyperglycaemia (e.g. steroids, antipsychotics) to the GP
  • Supports lifestyle counselling and weight management discussions at medicines reviews
  • Assists with structured medication reviews for patients with comorbidities that may affect glycaemia
👨‍⚕️
GP
  • Attends monthly QOF meeting; chairs review
  • Acts on results indicating progression to diabetes — diagnoses formally, moves patient to diabetes register, initiates management
  • Approves clinical exception reporting
  • Annual register accuracy review — add new diagnoses, remove incorrect entries
  • Reviews patients with persistent elevated results or comorbidities affecting glycaemia

Step-by-Step Process

1
Register Identification & Accuracy Check

The admin team runs the NDH register report at the start of each month. The GP Lead reviews register accuracy at least annually.

  • Run the NDH register report from the QOF dashboard
  • Identify patients with no HbA1c or FBG result in the past 12 months
  • Identify patients due a test within the next 6 weeks (proactive recall)
  • Flag any patients newly diagnosed with type 2 diabetes — remove from NDH register and add to diabetes register with correct SNOMED code
  • Flag any patients who should be on the register but are not (e.g. result of HbA1c 42–47 or FPG 5.5–6.9 coded without a register entry)
2
Patient Invitation

The admin team contacts patients due a blood test, using a structured recall process.

  • Send invitation letter and / or SMS to all patients with overdue or upcoming blood tests
  • Book a phlebotomy or nurse appointment for HbA1c or fasting blood glucose
  • Log the date of invitation in the patient record
  • If no response within 4 weeks: send a second invitation
  • If 3 contacts made without response: add to the monthly QOF meeting agenda for team review
3
Blood Test Completion

Either an HbA1c or a fasting blood glucose satisfies NDH002. Document the test using the correct SNOMED code on the day of the appointment.

  • Confirm which test is being used: HbA1c (non-fasting, preferred for most patients) or fasting blood glucose (if HbA1c is unreliable, e.g. haemoglobinopathy)
  • Take blood sample or issue request for external phlebotomy
  • Code the test using the correct SNOMED code on the same day
  • Provide brief lifestyle advice at the appointment — weight management, physical activity, dietary changes
4
Results Review & Action

The HCA or nurse reviews results and takes action based on outcome. Abnormal or concerning results are escalated to the GP on the same day.

  • HbA1c 42–47 mmol/mol or FPG 5.5–6.9 mmol/l (NDH range): code result, reinforce lifestyle advice, offer NHS DPP referral if not already enrolled, book next annual recall
  • HbA1c ≥48 mmol/mol or FPG ≥7.0 mmol/l (diabetes range): flag to GP or pharmacist immediately (same day) — formal diabetes diagnosis, register change, initiate management pathway
  • HbA1c <42 mmol/mol or FPG <5.5 mmol/l (normal range): code result, review with GP whether patient should remain on NDH register or be removed
  • All results coded in EMIS using correct SNOMED code on the day they are reviewed
5
NHS Diabetes Prevention Programme (NHS DPP) Referral

All eligible patients should be offered a referral to the NHS DPP. This is a behaviour-change programme shown to reduce the risk of developing type 2 diabetes.

✅ Eligible
  • Aged 18 or over
  • Blood test result demonstrating NDH in the preceding 12 months
  • History of Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM) — also eligible for self-referral
🚫 Not Eligible
  • Pregnant
  • Ever diagnosed with type 2 diabetes
  • Recorded as living with moderate or severe frailty
  • Active eating disorder
  • Bariatric surgery within the previous 2 years
  • Code the NHS DPP referral in EMIS on the day it is made
  • Digital programme options are available for patients who prefer remote participation
6
Coding & Register Maintenance

Accurate coding is essential for QOF reporting. All results and clinical actions must be coded using the correct SNOMED codes on the day they occur.

  • HbA1c result: code using standard HbA1c SNOMED code with numeric value
  • Fasting blood glucose result: code using fasting plasma glucose SNOMED code with numeric value
  • NHS DPP referral: code referral to diabetes prevention programme
  • Progression to diabetes: add to diabetes register (correct diabetes type code), remove NDH record if superseded
  • Return to normal range: discuss with GP whether to maintain or remove from NDH register; document decision in notes
7
Exception Reporting

Exceptions may be entered by any appropriate member of staff but must be approved by the GP, and only where clinically justified.

  • Patient has been informed of the recommendation but declines (patient declined)
  • Clinical contraindication documented and approved by the GP
  • Patient recently registered (within 3 months) — new patient exception
⚠️
Exception reporting must never be used to improve QOF scores without clinical justification. Commissioners may audit a random sample of exceptions — incorrect coding can result in financial claw-back.
4

Monitoring & QOF Meeting

📅
Monthly QOF Meeting The GP Lead, Pharmacist, Practice Manager and Admin Lead meet monthly to review the QOF dashboard, assess performance against targets, problem-solve barriers and agree actionable improvement plans for the following month.
Frequency Activity Owner
Monthly Run NDH register report; identify patients with overdue or upcoming HbA1c / FBG Admin Lead
Monthly QOF meeting — GP Lead, Pharmacist, PM and Admin Lead review dashboard, performance against 50–90% target, agree recall actions GP Lead (Chair)
Annually Full NDH register accuracy review — add new NDH diagnoses, confirm diabetes progressions are correctly coded, review patients with normalised results GP Lead
Monthly QOF meeting agenda to include patients with 3+ failed contacts — discuss and agree approach as a team Admin Lead + QOF Meeting
