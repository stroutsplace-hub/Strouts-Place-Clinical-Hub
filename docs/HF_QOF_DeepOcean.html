Standard Operating Procedure

Heart Failure
QOF 2025/26

Achieving targets for HF003, HF006, HF007 & HF008 — a guide for administrative and clinical staff at Strouts Place Medical Centre.

Strouts Place Medical Centre Version 1.0 Effective: April 2025 Review: March 2026 Owner: GP Lead
!

Why This Matters

⚠️
Scale & Impact Heart failure is the only major cardiovascular disease with increasing prevalence in England, and carries a poor prognosis. Structured, evidence-based management — including echocardiographic diagnosis, ACE-I/ARB and licensed beta-blocker therapy, and regular review — is proven to improve symptoms, reduce hospitalisation rates, and extend survival across all age groups.
ℹ️
Scope All indicators apply to patients on the Heart Failure register. This SOP covers the full pathway — from register accuracy and diagnosis confirmation through to annual review, medication optimisation, monitoring, and exception reporting. HF003 and HF006 apply only to patients with HFrEF (reduced ejection fraction / LVSD); HF007 and HF008 apply to all patients on the register. Ensure each patient has their HF type correctly coded in EMIS.
1

QOF Indicators at a Glance

Code Indicator Points Target Applies to
HF008 Diagnosis confirmed by echocardiogram or specialist assessment within 6 months before or after diagnosis; or within 6 months of registration if no prior confirmation exists 6 50–90% All HF patients — diagnoses on/after 1 Apr 2023
HF003 Currently treated with an ACE inhibitor (ACE-I) or Angiotensin II receptor blocker (ARB) 6 60–92% HFrEF / LVSD patients only
HF006 Currently treated with a beta-blocker licensed for heart failure 6 60–92% HFrEF / LVSD patients only
HF007 Review in the preceding 12 months including functional capacity assessment and medication review at maximal tolerated doses 7 50–90% All HF register patients
💡
HFrEF / HFpEF distinction — critical for HF003 and HF006 HF003 and HF006 apply only to patients with Heart Failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), defined as HF due to left ventricular systolic dysfunction (LVSD) or reduced ejection fraction (EF). Patients with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) are excluded from these two indicators. HF007 and HF008 apply to all patients on the HF register regardless of ejection fraction. The type of HF must be clearly coded in EMIS for accurate denominator calculation.

Roles & Responsibilities

🗂️
Practice Manager
  • Owns overall QOF performance for heart failure indicators
  • Chairs the monthly QOF meeting; reviews dashboard and agrees action plans
  • Coordinates recall campaigns with the admin team following each QOF meeting
  • Escalates persistent non-responders at the monthly QOF meeting
  • Ensures register accuracy is reviewed regularly with the GP Lead
📋
Admin / Reception Team
  • Sends invitation letters and SMS when directed following the monthly QOF meeting
  • Books annual HF review appointments with the appropriate clinician
  • Scans and codes echocardiogram reports and specialist letters on receipt — same day
  • Logs DNA attempts; adds patients with repeated non-response to the monthly QOF meeting agenda
  • Ensures new HF diagnosis letters from secondary care are flagged to the GP for register addition
🩺
HCA / Practice Nurse
  • Conducts the HF007 annual review using (RP) LTC Review Tower Hamlets CEG v1.4
  • Records functional capacity (NYHA class, exercise tolerance, breathlessness, oedema)
  • Records weight, blood pressure, heart rate and rhythm
  • Records smoking status and alcohol history (AUDIT-C)
  • Codes all review elements on the same day as the appointment
  • Flags any clinical deterioration to the Pharmacist or GP on the same day
💊
Pharmacist
  • Attends monthly QOF meeting with GP Lead, PM and Admin Lead
  • Conducts medication review — confirms and optimises ACE-I/ARB (HF003) and licensed beta-blocker (HF006)
  • Titrates therapy to maximum tolerated dose; documents any barriers
  • Arranges and reviews monitoring bloods (U&E, LFTs) after initiation or dose changes
  • Reviews diuretic dose at each appointment
  • Escalates to GP if clinical concerns are identified or therapy thresholds are not being met
👨‍⚕️
GP Lead
  • Chairs the monthly QOF meeting; reviews clinical data and performance
  • Maintains register accuracy — adds new diagnoses, removes incorrect entries
  • Approves clinical exception reporting
  • Acts on escalations from Pharmacist, HCA or Nurse
  • Reviews new HF diagnoses from secondary care — confirms HF type (HFrEF vs HFpEF) and codes appropriately
  • Considers referral to specialist HF team or cardiology for complex or deteriorating patients
  • Reviews patients with repeated DNA or declining attendance raised at the monthly QOF meeting

Step-by-Step Process

1
Register Accuracy & Indicator Review

The HF register and QOF dashboard are reviewed at the monthly QOF meeting by the GP Lead, Pharmacist and Practice Manager. Admin do not generate recall lists independently — all action plans are agreed at the QOF meeting and communicated to the admin team.

  • Review the HF register — confirm all patients have an accurate diagnosis and correct HF type coded (HFrEF vs HFpEF)
  • Identify patients with a new HF diagnosis (on/after 1 Apr 2023) with no echo or specialist confirmation recorded — flag for HF008
  • Identify HFrEF patients not currently prescribed ACE-I/ARB (HF003) or a licensed beta-blocker (HF006)
  • Identify all patients with no annual review in the preceding 12 months — flag for HF007
  • Admin team notified at or following the QOF meeting of patients to recall
2
Confirm Diagnosis — HF008 (Pharmacist or GP)

For all HF diagnoses made on or after 1 April 2023, the practice must confirm that an echocardiogram or specialist assessment has been completed. This applies regardless of whether the diagnosis was made at the practice or in secondary care.

  • New diagnosis at this practice: arrange echocardiogram or refer to cardiology / rapid-access HF clinic within 6 months of diagnosis. Ensure NT-proBNP is checked — if raised, refer urgently (2 weeks if very high; 6 weeks if raised)
  • Patient registered after diagnosis elsewhere: check for an existing echo report or specialist letter; if not present, arrange echo or specialist review within 6 months of registration
  • Acute HF presentation: NICE recommends echo within 48 hours of hospital admission for suspected acute HF with raised natriuretic peptides. On receipt of discharge documentation, admin codes the echo result same day and flags to GP for register review
  • All echo results and specialist letters must be coded in EMIS on the day of receipt, with the report date and relevant findings (EF, LVSD confirmed/excluded)
⚠️
Symptoms alone are insufficient Clinical symptoms and signs of HF are not sufficient to make a definitive QOF diagnosis. Cardiac dysfunction must be confirmed by echocardiography or specialist assessment. Ensure that diagnoses added to the register have documentary evidence of this confirmation.
3
Invite & Book Annual Review
  • Admin sends a personalised invitation letter or SMS
  • Book an annual HF review with HCA or Nurse — 20–30 minutes to allow full assessment
  • Advise the patient to bring a current medication list and any home blood pressure or weight monitoring records to the appointment
  • If no response after 2 weeks: send a second invitation. After a further 2 weeks: telephone outreach attempt
  • If still no response: add to the next monthly QOF meeting agenda for team review
4
Annual HF Review — HCA / Practice Nurse (HF007)

The review clinician completes the structured annual review using the (RP) LTC Review Tower Hamlets CEG v1.4 template in EMIS. All elements must be coded on the same day as the appointment.

💙  HF Annual Review (HF007)
  • ✓Functional capacity — NYHA class, exercise tolerance
  • ✓Weight and fluid status
  • ✓BMI
  • ✓Blood pressure
  • ✓Heart rate and pulse rhythm
  • ✓Smoking status
  • ✓Alcohol status (AUDIT-C)
  • ✓Frailty and palliative / end-of-life care needs considered where appropriate
  • ✓All findings coded same day in EMIS
⚠️
Functional capacity must be explicitly coded HF007 requires an assessment of functional capacity — this is a mandatory element. Recording a general "annual review" without a specific functional capacity code (e.g. NYHA classification or equivalent) will not satisfy the indicator. All clinical staff conducting this review must code the functional capacity assessment explicitly.
⚠️  Clinical Deterioration

If the patient shows signs of worsening HF — increased breathlessness, rapid weight gain (>2 kg in 3 days), increasing oedema, or new arrhythmia — flag to GP on the same day. Do not defer to the next scheduled appointment.

🚨  Urgent GP Review Needed

Inform the GP same day for: severe decompensation, possible acute HF, new AF / significant arrhythmia, or any clinical concern requiring urgent assessment. The GP decides on emergency management and follow-up.

5
Medication Optimisation — Pharmacist (HF003 & HF006)

Following the annual review, the Pharmacist conducts a structured medication review, with the goal of achieving maximum tolerated doses of first-line HFrEF therapies.

  • HF003 — ACE-I or ARB: Confirm the patient is currently prescribed and taking an ACE inhibitor (first line) or ARB (if ACE-I intolerant). If not prescribed and no documented contraindication: initiate therapy. If prescribed at a sub-therapeutic dose: up-titrate. Document any barriers to titration clearly.
  • HF006 — Licensed beta-blocker: Confirm the patient is currently prescribed and taking bisoprolol, carvedilol, or nebivolol (≥70 years, stable mild–moderate HF). If not prescribed and no documented contraindication: initiate at a very low dose. Titrate slowly over weeks to months. Document any barriers.
  • Arrange monitoring bloods (U&E, LFTs) after ACE-I/ARB initiation or dose increase; review results before further titration
  • Review diuretic dose — adjust for fluid status, renal function and symptoms
  • Escalate to GP Lead if therapy cannot be optimised due to clinical complexity, persistent side effects, or deteriorating renal function
  • Signpost to lifestyle support where relevant: smoking cessation, alcohol reduction, weight management, physical activity
💡
Titration is part of the indicator intent NICE NG106 requires that therapies are titrated upwards to the target or maximum tolerated dose. Prescribing at a sub-therapeutic dose without documenting clinical reasons for not titrating will not satisfy the clinical standard, even if the indicator is technically met. Always record current doses and the reason for any ceiling on titration.
6
Follow-up & Recall
  • Stable HF: schedule routine review within 6–12 months; annual QOF review must be completed within 12 months
  • After any medication initiation or dose change: arrange follow-up within 1–2 weeks to review symptoms, blood pressure, heart rate and monitoring bloods
  • Deteriorating or complex patients: shorten review intervals and consider referral to the specialist HF team or cardiology
  • Patients who DNA repeated appointments: escalate to the monthly QOF meeting; GP reviews clinical appropriateness of alternative outreach (e.g. home visit, telephone review, carer contact)
  • Consider exception reporting for patients who have declined after appropriate information and two documented invitation attempts — see Section 7
4

Clinical Guidance — Drug Therapy

💊  HF003 — ACE Inhibitor or ARB (HFrEF / LVSD)

NICE NG106 recommends ACE inhibitors as first-line therapy in all patients with HFrEF. ACE-I improve symptoms, reduce hospitalisation rates, and improve survival in all age groups. ARBs are used only in patients who are intolerant of ACE-I (typically due to cough or angioedema). Both therapies should be titrated upwards to target or maximum tolerated dose.

First line — ACE Inhibitor
Ramipril Lisinopril Enalapril Perindopril

Titrate to target dose per BNF. Monitor U&E and BP at initiation and each dose increase.

If ACE-I intolerant — ARB
Candesartan Valsartan Losartan

Use only if ACE-I intolerant. Document the reason for switching. Titrate to target dose.

Do not combine

Do not prescribe ACE-I and ARB together in HF — this increases the risk of hyperkalaemia and renal impairment without additional benefit (NICE NG106).

🧪
Monitoring required — Check U&E and BP at baseline, 1–2 weeks after initiation, 1–2 weeks after each dose increase, and at least annually when stable. Hyperkalaemia (>5.5 mmol/L) or a significant fall in eGFR requires dose reduction or GP review.
💊  HF006 — Beta-blocker Licensed for Heart Failure (HFrEF / LVSD)

NICE NG106 recommends licensed beta-blockers as first-line therapy alongside ACE-I/ARB in all patients with HFrEF. Beta-blockers must be initiated at a very low dose and titrated slowly over weeks to months. Symptoms may worsen initially, requiring adjustment of concomitant diuretic therapy. This is expected and does not necessarily indicate treatment failure.

✅ Licensed for HF — use these
Bisoprolol Carvedilol Nebivolol (≥70 yrs)

Start low, go slow. Titrate at 2-week intervals if tolerated. Target doses per BNF / NICE NG106.

🚫 Not licensed for HF — do not switch
Atenolol Metoprolol Propranolol Sotalol

Patients already on an unlicensed beta-blocker before HF diagnosis are excluded from HF006 denominator. Do not change their drug solely to meet QOF.

When not to withhold beta-blockers

NICE NG106 states that beta-blockers should not be withheld solely because of: older age, peripheral vascular disease (PVD), erectile dysfunction (ED), diabetes mellitus, interstitial pulmonary disease, or COPD without reversibility. The only clear contraindications are asthma and reversible airways obstruction — these groups were excluded from the clinical trials underpinning this recommendation.

📅  Review Frequency — NICE NG106
Stable HF

Routine review within 6–12 months. Annual QOF review must be completed within 12 months (HF007).

After Medication Change

Review within days to 2 weeks. Check BP, heart rate, symptoms and monitoring bloods.

Deteriorating / Complex

Shorten interval significantly. Consider specialist HF team referral. Assess for frailty and palliative / end-of-life needs.

Monitoring Requirements

Investigation Frequency Indication / Notes Responsible
U&E (renal function, electrolytes) 1–2 weeks post change 6-monthly stable After initiation or dose increase of ACE-I, ARB or diuretic. At least 6-monthly when stable. Check for hyperkalaemia (>5.5) and eGFR decline. Pharmacist / GP
LFTs Annually Routine annual monitoring. More frequently if liver disease or amiodarone prescribed. Pharmacist / GP
FBC Annually Check for anaemia (common in HF, worsens prognosis) and iron deficiency. GP / Pharmacist
TFTs Annually Particularly important if patient is on amiodarone. Thyroid dysfunction can worsen or precipitate HF. GP / Nurse
Blood pressure 1–2 weeks post change 6-monthly At every clinical contact. After any antihypertensive or diuretic change. Target BP in HF is guided by clinical tolerance, not a fixed QOF threshold. HCA / Nurse / Pharmacist / GP
Heart rate & rhythm Each review At every structured review. ECG if new AF, significant bradycardia (<60), or tachycardia (>100) unexplained by clinical condition. HCA / Nurse
Weight Each review Advise patients to weigh daily at home if able. >2 kg gain in 3 days indicates fluid retention — increase diuretic or contact practice as agreed in self-management plan. HCA / Nurse
NT-proBNP / BNP As clinically indicated Not a routine annual QOF requirement, but useful to guide diagnosis (HF008) and detect deterioration. Obtain on new/suspected diagnosis to guide referral urgency. GP
⚠️
Monitoring triggers action All monitoring results must be reviewed by a clinician and acted upon. Abnormal results should not be left without a documented clinical response.

Exception Reporting

Exception reporting allows practices to exclude individual patients from specific QOF indicators where it would be clinically inappropriate or impossible to include them. Exceptions must be coded correctly in EMIS and must be justified by a clear clinical or administrative reason.

🩺  Clinical Exceptions
  • →Documented contraindication to the medication (e.g. asthma for beta-blockers; bilateral renal artery stenosis for ACE-I)
  • →Adverse reaction or intolerance clearly documented in the patient record
  • →Comorbidity that makes the intervention clinically inappropriate (e.g. end-stage renal disease for ACE-I; severe bradycardia for beta-blockers)
  • →Palliative care / end-of-life care agreed — treatment de-escalation is appropriate and documented
  • →Specialist instruction not to prescribe — documented in a clinic letter or correspondence
🙋  Patient Preference / Administrative Exceptions
  • →Patient declined therapy after receiving information about the benefits and risks — discussion documented
  • →Patient declined annual review after two documented invitation attempts — both attempts must be recorded in EMIS before this exception can be applied
  • →Patient newly registered — insufficient time to complete the required intervention within the measurement window
  • →Use the correct EMIS exception code — distinguish between clinical contraindication and patient preference / non-attendance; these are counted separately in QOF reporting
ℹ️
HF006 — Unlicensed beta-blocker exclusion Patients who were already taking a beta-blocker that is not licensed for heart failure (e.g. atenolol, metoprolol) at the time of their HF diagnosis are automatically excluded from the HF006 denominator. This exclusion is built into the QOF business rules — it is not an exception you need to apply. Do not change their beta-blocker solely to meet the indicator.
